Dessert One

Knafeh~ pronounced kuh-NAY-fuh or kuh-NAF-eh

Palestinian Knafeh

The most iconic Palestinian dessert — shredded kataifi dough layered with stretchy cheese, baked golden, and soaked in fragrant sugar syrup.

Prep
15 min
Cook
30 min
Total
45 min
Serves
8
Category
Dessert
Cuisine
Palestinian
Method
Baking

Knafeh (also spelled knafah, kunafa, kinafeh, or kanafih) is considered the most traditional and iconic dessert not only in Palestine but across the entire Middle East. It is slightly crunchy on the outside, cheesy and gooey on the inside — a unique combination of textures and flavors that is loved by children and adults alike.

It is believed that knafeh originated in Nablus, a Palestinian city in the West Bank, famous for Knafeh Nabulsi — made with local Nabulsi cheese, thin strands of shredded dough, and aromatic sugar syrup. There are different regional variations, but the most common types are: Khishneh (rough), Na'meh (fine), and Mabroumeh (twined).

Knafeh is the dessert Palestinians reach for to celebrate any occasion — a new job, a graduation, a promotion, or simply to share something sweet with family and friends.

Ingredients

Dough & Butter

  • ½ lb (8 oz) kataifi dough, thawed (half a standard bag)
  • ½ cup unsalted butter or ghee, melted
  • Orange food coloring (optional, for traditional color)

Cheese Filling

  • 1 lb unsalted white sweet cheese or akawi cheese, shredded or cut into small pieces

To Finish

  • 1 cup warm sugar syrup (ater), plus extra to serve
  • ¼ cup crushed pistachios, for garnishing

Sugar Syrup (Ater)

  • 2 cups granulated sugar
  • 1 cup water
  • 1 teaspoon lemon juice
  • 1 teaspoon rose water or orange blossom water

Instructions

Make the Sugar Syrup

  1. Combine sugar and water in a saucepan over medium heat, stirring until the sugar dissolves.
  2. Add the lemon juice and bring to a gentle boil. Simmer for 10 minutes until slightly thickened.
  3. Remove from heat and stir in rose water or orange blossom water. Set aside to keep warm.

Prepare the Dough

  1. Remove the frozen kataifi dough from the freezer and allow it to thaw for 2–3 hours before using.
  2. Preheat the oven to 350°F (175°C).
  3. Place the kataifi dough in a food processor and pulse to shred, or shred by hand with a knife to your preferred texture — rough for khishneh, finer for na'meh.
  4. Transfer the shredded dough to a large bowl. Pour the melted butter and food coloring (if using) over it.
  5. Using your hands, massage the butter thoroughly into the dough until everything is evenly coated and well integrated.

Assemble the Knafeh

  1. Grease a 9-inch round baking pan with butter. (Use an 18-inch pan if using the full bag of dough.)
  2. Spread the majority of the buttered dough evenly across the bottom of the pan, reserving about ¼ for the top layer. Press it down firmly.
  3. Spread the shredded cheese evenly over the dough layer. Press down firmly with the palm of your hand or the flat bottom of a glass.
  4. Spread the remaining dough over the cheese and press down firmly again.

Bake, Flip & Serve

  1. Bake for 20–30 minutes, until the edges are deeply golden and crispy and the cheese is fully melted. Check the cheese with a fork to confirm.
  2. Remove from the oven. Place a serving platter (same size or slightly larger) firmly over the pan, then carefully flip in one confident motion.
  3. Immediately drizzle ½ to ¾ of the warm sugar syrup over the entire surface of the knafeh. Reserve the rest for serving on the side.
  4. Sprinkle generously with crushed pistachios.
  5. Cut into pieces and serve hot.

Substitutions & Variations

  • For a nut-filled version (knafeh bil joz), replace the cheese with 2 cups of coarsely chopped walnuts mixed with 1 tablespoon of sugar and 1 teaspoon of cinnamon. Layer and bake using the same method.
  • If you can't find akawi or sweet white cheese, use half fresh mozzarella and half ricotta. This maintains a soft, stretchy texture once baked.
  • Akawi cheese is salty by nature — if using it, soak the cheese in cold water for several hours before using to draw out the salt.
  • If you're not comfortable flipping, skip it — layer the dough thin on the bottom and thicker on top of the cheese, then cut and serve directly from the baking pan.

Pro Tips

  • Use high quality butter or ghee — the amount and quality make a significant difference in the final taste.
  • Be generous with the cheese. A good stretch when cutting is the hallmark of a well-made knafeh.
  • Knafeh must be served hot. If you made the sugar syrup ahead of time, rewarm it on the stovetop or briefly in the microwave before drizzling.
  • Shred the dough to your preferred texture — rough for khishneh, fine for na'meh, or anywhere in between.
  • Press each layer down very firmly before baking — this helps the knafeh hold together cleanly when flipped.
  • Knafeh can be assembled and stored in the fridge a full day before baking — assemble, cover, and refrigerate, then bake when ready.
  • Unbaked knafeh can also be frozen in the baking pan for up to 3 months. Bake straight from frozen, adding extra time as needed.

Storage & Reheating

  • Knafeh tastes best served fresh and hot straight from the oven.
  • Store leftovers in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 2 days.
  • To reheat, microwave until warm and add an extra drizzle of warmed sugar syrup to restore moisture.
  • Unbaked knafeh can be wrapped well and frozen in the baking tray for up to 3 months.
